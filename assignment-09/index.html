This is it. You have decided to take the plunge into the unknown — Linux. I applaud your bravery. But which distribution (or "distro") should you choose? Ubuntu is often recommended to first-timers, Arch seems way to advanced for you, and you have no idea what a "Gentoo" even is. If I may, I offer an alternative suggestion: Fedora.

Why Fedora? Well, it is an excelent mix of the end-user simplicity and stability of Ubuntu & the speed and freedom of Arch:

  • Fedora uses the same GNOME desktop environment as Ubuntu.
  • Fedora does not come pre-installed with Snap, a controversial sandboxed package manager developed by Canonical, the parent company of Ubuntu.
    • If you still want sandboxed packages, I recommend using Flatpak instead.
  • Fedora uses a newer kernel version by default and more modern packages than Ubuntu, but not so modern as to compromise stability.
  • Fresh Fedora installs are often lighter than fresh Ubuntu installs.

Have I convinced you? Great. No time like the present to install it then!

Requirements

  1. A reasonably-modern, 64-bit PC with at least 4 GB of RAM and at least 40 GB of SSD-based storage. Fedora Workstation supports both x86_64 and ARM64 processor architectures.
    Side note, the naming conventions for processor architectures can get confusing in Linux. Here's a quick cheat-sheet: (Windows name = Linux/Unix name)
    • x86 = i386  —  named after the Intel 386 processor, the first 32-bit processor
    • x86_64 = amd64  —  there is far too much history to summarize here
    • ARM32 = armhf / arm32  —  I know there is a difference, but I have no idea what it is
    • ARM64 = arm64 / aarch64  —  While technically different, they are practically identical (and yes, this double-naming does cause problems)
  2. A copy of Fedora Linux downloaded from the Fedora Project website. I recommend going with Fedora Workstation, as it is intended for end-users' computers. Make sure you download the correct version for your computer's architecture.
  3. A reasonably-sized USB flash drive – USB 2.0 and 16 GB is probably fine.

Pre-Installation

Now that you have your flash drive prepared, it's time to make use of it.

  1. Download and install Etcher. This is a free and easy to use flash drive flashing tool.
  2. Open Etcher, select your flash drive (make sure it is the correct device!), select the Fedora Workstation ISO you downloaded earlier, and click Flash!
  3. Once that is done, you can safely remove the flash drive. Ignore any warnings about incompatible file systems.
  4. Plug in the flash drive to any available USB port. Plugging directly into the device or through a powered hub will work, but plugging into an unpowered hub (such as a keyboard) will not.
  5. Boot your computer into the boot picker. Tom's Hardware has a great article on how to do this.
  6. Select the flash drive. It may be called something like "Generic Mass Storage Device" or, if the drive manufacturer was feeling fancy, it may contain the manufacturer's name.
  7. If presented with another bootloader, this is likely something called Grub. You shouldn't need to worry about what exactly is going on here, so just press enter.

Installation

At this point, you should be presented with a friendly installation wizard. This should walk you through all the steps to get this awesome OS installed, but I must give you some tips:

  • Make sure the computer has power throughout the entire process.
  • Leave the flash drive plugged into the computer until it explicitly tells you to remove it. Early removal is likely to result in a borked (aka broken, if only slightly) install.
  • If this is your first time, I recommend only installing Linux on non-primary devices, i.e. not one you or anyone else relies on to do their work. It is absolutely possible to dual-boot Linux and Windows or macOS, but it is beyond the scope of this walkthrough.
  • When presented with the partitioning tool, be EXTREMELY CAREFUL with what you select. While data recovery from accidental partitioning is possible, it can only be done with unencrypted drives, and even it's a huge pain in the [insert expletive of choice]. I have completely and irreversibly destoryed countless installs of macOS, Windows, and Linux because of careless partitioning — so many so that it became a bit of a curse word in my house.

Post-Installation

If the installer successfully finished its tasks and rebooted into Fedora Linux, congradulations! You have officially taken your first steps into the wild world of Linux.

Once logged in, you will be presented with a quite lovely default wallpaper. To access your applications, either:

  • swipe up twice on the trackpad with three fingers,
  • press the Super key (should be either the Windows key or ⌘ key), then click the "waffle" on the right side of the dock, or
  • click "Activities" in the top-left corner of the display, then click the "waffle" on the right side of the dock.

From here, I recommend just exploring what Fedora has to offer. I made another page with some common commands used in Fedora (and Linux, more broadly) to help you get familiar with the terminal. If you're intereseted, I've embedded a video detailing some post-install items you can do below.
